1. DNA Replication: A Deep Dive

Copying the Master Blueprint for Exam Preparation

The Core Concept

Before a cell can divide, it must duplicate its entire genome. This process, DNA Replication, ensures that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical set of genetic instructions.

Replication is semi-conservative: the original double helix unwinds, and each of the two parental strands serves as a template for a new, complementary strand. The result is two identical DNA molecules, each consisting of one "old" strand and one "new" strand.

The Replication Fork

Replication doesn't happen all at once. It occurs at a Y-shaped structure called the Replication Fork, where the DNA is actively being unwound and copied. This fork moves along the DNA as replication proceeds.

The entire complex of enzymes and proteins at the fork is called the Replisome.

Part 2: The Process - Step-by-Step

Step 1: Initiation at oriC

Replication begins at a single, unique site called the origin of replication (oriC). This site contains specific sequences: four 9-bp repeats and three 13-bp (AT-rich) repeats.

The DnaA protein (the initiator) binds to the 9-bp repeats, causing the DNA to wrap around the protein complex. This strain forces the AT-rich 13-bp repeats to open. DnaC (the loader) then escorts DnaB (Helicase) into this open bubble. Two helicases are loaded, one for each direction, forming two replication forks. This is called theta-replication because the structure resembles the Greek letter theta (θ).

Step 2: Elongation (The Fork)

As Helicase unwinds the DNA, DNA Gyrase works ahead to relieve supercoiling, and SSB proteins coat the single strands.

Leading Strand (Continuous): Primase makes *one* RNA primer. DNA Pol III (held by its sliding clamp) binds and synthesizes a new strand continuously towards the fork.

Lagging Strand (Discontinuous): This strand runs the wrong way. The Primosome (PriA + Primase) must make a *new* RNA primer every 1000-2000 bases. DNA Pol III then synthesizes a short piece, an Okazaki fragment, until it hits the next primer. The Pol III complex then detaches and re-loads at the newest primer.

Step 3: Completing the Lagging Strand

After the fork passes, the lagging strand is a mess of DNA fragments and RNA primers.

1. RNase H removes most of the RNA primer.
2. DNA Polymerase I binds to the nick. Its 5'→3' exonuclease activity removes the rest of the RNA primer, while its 5'→3' polymerase activity fills the gap with DNA.
3. DNA Ligase seals the final phosphate-sugar bond (the "nick"), joining the fragments into a single, complete strand.

Part 3: Control of Initiation & Termination

Control: DNA Methylation

How does the cell prevent re-replication? By using DNA methylation.

The Dam methylase adds methyl groups to the 'A' in all GATC sequences. After replication, the old strand is methylated, but the new strand is not. This is hemi-methylated DNA.

The hemi-methylated oriC is "sequestered" by the SeqA protein, which binds it to the cell membrane. This physically hides the origin, preventing DnaA from binding again until the Dam methylase can catch up and fully methylate the new strand.

Termination & Decatenation

The two forks move in opposite directions and meet at the terminus region. This region contains Ter sites.

The Tus protein binds to the Ter sites and acts as a one-way gate, stopping the helicase from one direction but allowing the other to arrive.

The two new circular chromosomes are often linked like rings in a chain (catenated). Topoisomerase IV cuts one chromosome, passes the other through the break, and reseals it to separate them (decatenation).

Eukaryotic Replication: The Human Model

Eukaryotic replication follows the same core principles (semi-conservative, 5'→3' synthesis, leading/lagging strands) but is more complex. It solves two main problems: Scale and Linear Ends.

Part 1: The Eukaryotic Problems & Solutions

Problem 1: Scale

Human chromosomes are enormous. Replicating from one origin would take weeks.

Solution: Multiple Origins of Replication. Eukaryotic chromosomes have 10,000 - 100,000 origins. Replication begins at all of them simultaneously, forming "replication bubbles" that grow and merge, rapidly copying the entire chromosome.

Problem 2: Linear Ends

When the final RNA primer is removed from the 5' end of the lagging strand, DNA Pol I/δ cannot fill the gap (it needs a 3'-OH to add to). This is the End-Replication Problem, and it means the chromosome would get shorter with every division.

Solution: Telomeres & Telomerase.
Telomeres are long, repetitive DNA sequences (TTAGGG in humans) at the ends of chromosomes that act as a buffer.
Telomerase is a special enzyme (a reverse transcriptase) that carries its *own RNA template*. It binds to the 3' end and extends it by adding more TTAGGG repeats, allowing a new primer to be laid down to fill the gap.

Part 3: Control of Initiation (The Cell Cycle)

In eukaryotes, replication is strictly tied to the Cell Cycle.

* G1 Phase: The cell grows.
* S Phase: DNA synthesis (replication) occurs.
* G2 Phase: The cell prepares for division.
* M Phase: Mitosis (cell division) occurs.

To ensure origins fire "once and only once" per S-phase, a Replication Licensing Factor (RLF) binds to all origins during G1. When S-phase begins, the RLF is displaced or destroyed, preventing that origin from firing again until the *next* G1 phase.

Replication: Exam Prep & Core Concepts

The Concept of the Replicon

A replicon is any piece of DNA that can replicate itself. It *must* contain an origin of replication.

* A bacterial chromosome is a single replicon. * A eukaryotic chromosome contains *many* replicons. * Plasmids and many viruses are also replicons.

In bacteria, replicons are usually circular. This is because linear DNA is seen as "broken" and is rapidly destroyed by cellular enzymes called exonucleases.

Supplying the dNTPs (Precursors)

The "bricks" for DNA (dATP, dGTP, dCTP, dTTP) are made from the "bricks" for RNA (ADP, GDP, CDP, UDP) by the enzyme Ribonucleotide Reductase.

To make Thymine (dTTP), Thymidylate Synthetase adds a methyl group to dUMP. This reaction requires Tetrahydrofolate (THF).

Clinical Targets (Exam Gold):
* Sulfonamides: Antibiotics that block folate *synthesis* in bacteria.
* Trimethoprim: Antibiotic that blocks Dihydrofolate Reductase (recycles THF) in *bacteria*.
* Methotrexate: Chemo drug that blocks Dihydrofolate Reductase in *humans* to stop cancer cell replication.

2. PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

Replication in a Test Tube

Developed by Kary Mullis in 1983, PCR is a technique to amplify a specific segment of DNA exponentially, creating millions of copies from just a few. It mimics DNA replication but uses temperature cycles to control the process.

The "Recipe" (Components)

DNA Template

The source DNA containing the target sequence to be amplified.

DNA Polymerase

A heat-stable enzyme. Taq polymerase is common, but Pfu polymerase is used for high-fidelity (fewer errors) applications.

Primers (Forward & Reverse)

Short, synthetic DNA strands that define the start and end of the target sequence. They are complementary to the 3' ends of the target.

dNTPs (Nucleotides)

The building blocks (A, T, C, G) for the new DNA strands.

Buffer with Mg2+

Provides the optimal chemical environment. Mg2+ is a crucial co-factor for the polymerase.

The "Program" (The Cycle)

1. Denaturation (~94°C)

The mixture is heated to separate the double-stranded DNA into two single strands.

2. Annealing (~54°C)

The temperature is lowered, allowing the primers to hybridize (bind) to their complementary sequences on the single strands.

3. Extension (~72°C)

The temperature is raised to the optimal level for Taq polymerase, which adds dNTPs to extend the primers and synthesize new strands.

This cycle is repeated 30-40 times, resulting in an exponential amplification (2n) of the target DNA.

PCR: Pro-Tips & Applications

Pro-Tip: Designing Perfect Primers

A successful PCR depends on good primers. They should be:

  • Specific Length: Typically 18-25 nucleotides.
  • Balanced: About 40-60% GC content (Guanine/Cytosine) for good binding.
  • Not Self-Complementary: Must avoid binding to themselves (hairpins) or each other (primer-dimers).

Common Applications

Forensics: Amplifying tiny DNA samples from a crime scene for identification.
Disease Diagnosis: Detecting the presence of viral or bacterial DNA (like for COVID-19 or HIV).
Research: Cloning genes, studying gene expression, and sequencing ancient DNA.

3. Transcription: A Deep Dive

Reading the Blueprint (DNA to RNA)

Core Concept: The RNA Family

Not all RNA is destined to become protein. There are three main types:

  • mRNA (Messenger): The "instructions" that get translated (5-10%).
  • tRNA (Transfer): The "adaptor" that carries amino acids (10-20%).
  • rRNA (Ribosomal): The "factory" component; part of the ribosome (60-80%).

Core Concept: Template vs. Coding

RNA Polymerase reads the Template Strand (3'→5') to synthesize a new RNA strand (5'→3'). The other DNA strand, the Coding Strand, has the *same* sequence as the new RNA (just with T instead of U).

Core Concept: Cistrons

A "cistron" is a gene. Prokaryotic mRNA is often polycistronic (one mRNA has instructions for several proteins). Eukaryotic mRNA is monocistronic (one mRNA = one protein).

Prokaryotic Transcription

In bacteria, transcription is a rapid process performed by a single, multi-part enzyme. It occurs in the cytoplasm, and translation can begin *before* transcription is even finished.

The Prokaryotic RNA Polymerase

The "Holoenzyme" is the complete, active enzyme. It has two parts:

1. The Core Enzyme (α2ββ'ω)
  • α (alpha): Determines the DNA to be transcribed.
  • β (beta): Catalyzes polymerization.
  • β' (beta-prime): Binds & opens the DNA template.
2. The σ (Sigma) Factor
  • The "specificity" factor. It recognizes and binds to promoter sequences.
  • Different σ-factors recognize different promoters (e.g., heat shock genes).

The Process - Step-by-Step

1. Initiation

The Holoenzyme (with σ-factor) scans the DNA until the σ-factor recognizes a promoter. Promoters have two key consensus sequences: the -10 sequence (Pribnow box) and the -35 sequence.

The enzyme first binds to form a closed complex. It then unwinds the DNA at the -10 box to form an open complex, creating the "transcription bubble."

2. Elongation

After synthesizing about 10 RNA bases, the σ-factor is released (promoter clearance). The Core Enzyme now moves along the template strand, synthesizing RNA in a 5'→3' direction, maintaining an 8-bp RNA-DNA hybrid inside the bubble.

3. Termination

The enzyme stops at a terminator signal. There are two types:

  • Rho-independent: The RNA forms a G-C rich hairpin loop, followed by a weak poly-U tail. This structure stalls the polymerase and pulls the RNA out.
  • Rho-dependent: A "Rho" protein (an ATP-dependent helicase) binds to the new RNA, travels up to the stalled polymerase, and unwinds the RNA-DNA hybrid, kicking the polymerase off.

Eukaryotic Transcription

Eukaryotic transcription is far more complex. It happens in the nucleus, and the product (hnRNA) must be heavily modified before it can be exported to the cytoplasm for translation.

The Process: Initiation with Pol II

RNA Pol II (which makes mRNA) cannot bind to DNA on its own. It requires a team of General Transcription Factors (TFs).

Promoters & Factors

The core promoter contains a TATA box (around -25) and an Initiator element.

The process begins when TFs (like TFIID) bind to the TATA box. This recruits other factors and finally RNA Pol II itself, forming a massive pre-initiation complex.

Enhancers & Silencers

Gene expression is controlled by DNA sequences that can be thousands of bases away.

* Enhancers: Bind *activator* proteins to *increase* transcription.
* Silencers: Bind *repressor* proteins to *decrease* transcription.

These elements, through DNA looping, interact with the promoter complex to regulate the rate of initiation.

Post-Transcriptional Modification

In eukaryotes, the initial "primary transcript" (or hnRNA) is not functional. It must undergo "RNA processing" in the nucleus to become a mature, stable mRNA ready for export.

1. 5' Capping

An unusual 7-methylguanosine cap is added to the 5' end of the RNA via a rare 5'-to-5' triphosphate linkage. This cap is essential for:
* Protecting the mRNA from degradation.
* Helping the ribosome bind during translation.

2. 3' Polyadenylation

The 3' end is cleaved, and an enzyme called Poly(A) Polymerase adds a long chain (40-200) of adenine bases, forming the Poly(A) Tail. This tail:
* Increases the stability of the mRNA.
* Is required for its export from the nucleus.

3. RNA Splicing

Eukaryotic genes are "split." They contain coding regions (Exons) interrupted by non-coding regions (Introns).

The Spliceosome, a large complex made of snRNPs (small nuclear ribonucleoproteins), cuts out the introns and joins the exons together, forming a lariat structure from the removed intron.

Processing of other RNAs

tRNA Modification

tRNA transcripts are also processed. This involves cleaving sequences from the 5' and 3' ends, (sometimes) splicing, and extensive base modification (e.g., methylation, reduction) to create the characteristic D and Ψ loops.

rRNA Modification

In the nucleolus, a large 45S pre-rRNA transcript is synthesized by Pol I. This single transcript is then methylated and cleaved by nucleases into the final 18S, 5.8S, and 28S rRNAs that form the core of the ribosome.

4. Translation: A Deep Dive

Building the Machine (RNA to Protein)

Translation is the final, complex process of synthesizing a polypeptide (protein) from an mRNA template. This process is orchestrated by the ribosome and requires a team of helper molecules.

Part 1: The "Tools" for Building

mRNA (Messenger RNA)

The "Instructions." Carries the genetic code in 3-letter codons.

tRNA (Transfer RNA)

The "Adaptor." One end has an anticodon to read the codon; the other carries the correct amino acid.

Ribosome (rRNA + Protein)

The "Factory." Has a large and small subunit, and three sites: A (Aminoacyl), P (Peptidyl), and E (Exit).

Part 2: Preparing for Translation - Charging the tRNA

Aminoacyl-tRNA Synthetase

This enzyme is the *true* translator. It ensures the correct amino acid is attached to the correct tRNA. This two-step process uses ATP and is incredibly specific.

  • Step 1 (Activation): Amino Acid + ATP → Aminoacyl-AMP + PPi
  • Step 2 (Charging): Aminoacyl-AMP + tRNA → Aminoacyl-tRNA + AMP

Prokaryotic Translation: Step-by-Step

1. Initiation

The 30S (small) subunit, along with IF1 and IF3, binds to the mRNA. The Shine-Dalgarno sequence on the mRNA base-pairs with the 16S rRNA of the 30S subunit, perfectly positioning it.

IF2 (bound to GTP) escorts the initiator fMet-tRNA to the P site. The 50S (large) subunit joins, GTP is hydrolyzed, and all IFs are released. The 70S initiation complex is now ready.

2. Elongation

This is a 3-step cycle, driven by GTP.

1. Binding: EF-Tu (with GTP) escorts the next charged tRNA to the A site.
2. Peptidyl Transfer: The 23S rRNA (a Ribozyme!) in the large subunit catalyzes a peptide bond. The growing polypeptide is transferred from the P-site tRNA to the A-site tRNA.
3. Translocation: EF-G (with GTP) binds, causing the ribosome to shift one codon over. The now-empty tRNA moves to the E site and exits. The tRNA holding the polypeptide moves into the P site. The A site is now open for the next cycle.

3. Termination

When a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) enters the A site, no tRNA matches.

Instead, a Release Factor (RF1 or RF2) binds. This causes the ribosome to transfer the polypeptide to a water molecule, releasing it.

RF3 then causes the release factors and ribosome to dissociate, ready to start again.

Eukaryotic Translation: Key Differences

Initiation (The Main Difference)

Eukaryotes use an 80S ribosome and *many* more initiation factors (eIFs).

The 40S (small) subunit *first* binds the initiator Met-tRNA (escorted by eIF2). This complex is then recruited to the 5' cap of the mRNA by other eIFs.

The entire complex then scans down the mRNA until it finds the first AUG in a good Kozak sequence (e.g., CAAAAUG). This is where the 60S subunit joins and translation begins.

Elongation & Termination

The process is nearly identical to prokaryotes, but the helper proteins have different names.

* Elongation: Uses eEF1 (like EF-Tu) and eEF2 (like EF-G).
* Termination: A single release factor, eRF1, recognizes all three stop codons.

Exam Prep: Inhibitors & Key Concepts

Inhibitors of Transcription

These are classic exam questions:

  • Rifampicin: (Antibiotic) Binds to the β-subunit of prokaryotic RNA Polymerase. Used to treat Tuberculosis.
  • Actinomycin D: Binds to DNA and blocks the movement of *all* RNA Polymerases (prokaryotic and eukaryotic). A research tool.
  • α-Amanitin: (From "Death Cap" mushrooms) A deadly toxin that inhibits eukaryotic RNA Polymerase II.

Breaking the Dogma: Reverse Transcription

The central dogma (DNA → RNA → Protein) is not absolute. Retroviruses (like HIV) use an enzyme called Reverse Transcriptase to synthesize DNA *from* an RNA template (RNA → DNA). This viral DNA can then integrate into the host's genome.
