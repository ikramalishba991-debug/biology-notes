1. DNA Replication

Copying the Master Blueprint

The Process

DNA replication is semi-conservative, meaning each new DNA molecule contains one old strand and one new strand. This process occurs in two main stages at a Y-shaped structure called the replication fork.

  • Strand Separation: Unwinding the double helix.
  • Synthesis: Building two new complementary strands.

Key Concept: The Lagging Strand

DNA Polymerase can only build in the 5' to 3' direction. This is fine for one strand (the leading strand), but the other strand (the lagging strand) must be synthesized backwards in small pieces called Okazaki fragments. These fragments are later stitched together by DNA ligase.

The Replication Machinery

DNA Helicase

The "Unzipper." It unwinds the DNA double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds, creating the replication fork.

DNA Gyrase (Topoisomerase)

The "Swivel." It relieves supercoiling and topological stress ahead of the helicase by cutting and resealing the DNA.

SSB Proteins

The "Shields." Single-Strand Binding proteins coat the separated strands to prevent them from re-annealing.

Primase (DnaG)

The "Ignition." It synthesizes short RNA primers because DNA Polymerase cannot start a new strand from scratch.

DNA Polymerase III (Pol III)

The "Builder." The main replication enzyme. It adds nucleotides in the 5' to 3' direction, using the template. It has proofreading ability.

DNA Polymerase I (Pol I)

The "Cleanup Crew." It removes the RNA primers from the lagging strand and replaces them with DNA.

DNA Ligase

The "Glue." It joins the Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand by sealing the nicks in the sugar-phosphate backbone.

Tidbit: Eukaryotic vs. Prokaryotic

  • Prokaryotic Initiation: Bacteria (like E. coli) have one origin of replication (`oriC`), where the DnaA protein binds to start the process.
  • Eukaryotic Initiation: Eukaryotes have multiple origins to replicate their much larger genomes quickly.
  • Eukaryotic Enzymes: Eukaryotes use different polymerases (Pol α for priming, Pol δ for elongation) and a sliding clamp called PCNA.
  • Eukaryotic Ends: Eukaryotes have linear chromosomes and use Telomerase to maintain the ends (telomeres) and prevent shortening.

Pro-Tip: Building the Blocks

Where do dATP, dGTP, dCTP, and dTTP come from? The cell makes them from their RNA counterparts (ADP, GDP, CDP, UDP) using an enzyme called Ribonucleotide Reductase. This enzyme is a key checkpoint for controlling cell division.

Pro-Tip: Termination & Untangling

In bacteria, replication forks are stopped at Terminator sites (`terC`) by the Tus protein. The two new circular chromosomes are often interlinked (catenated). Topoisomerase IV acts like a molecular magician, cutting one circle, passing the other through, and resealing it to separate them.

2. PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

Replication in a Test Tube

Developed by Kary Mullis in 1983, PCR is a technique to amplify a specific segment of DNA exponentially, creating millions of copies from just a few. It mimics DNA replication but uses temperature cycles to control the process.

The "Recipe" (Components)

DNA Template

The source DNA containing the target sequence to be amplified.

DNA Polymerase

A heat-stable enzyme. Taq polymerase is common, but Pfu polymerase is used for high-fidelity (fewer errors) applications.

Primers (Forward & Reverse)

Short, synthetic DNA strands that define the start and end of the target sequence. They are complementary to the 3' ends of the target.

dNTPs (Nucleotides)

The building blocks (A, T, C, G) for the new DNA strands.

Buffer with Mg2+

Provides the optimal chemical environment. Mg2+ is a crucial co-factor for the polymerase.

The "Program" (The Cycle)

1. Denaturation (~94°C)

The mixture is heated to separate the double-stranded DNA into two single strands.

2. Annealing (~54°C)

The temperature is lowered, allowing the primers to hybridize (bind) to their complementary sequences on the single strands.

3. Extension (~72°C)

The temperature is raised to the optimal level for Taq polymerase, which adds dNTPs to extend the primers and synthesize new strands.

This cycle is repeated 30-40 times, resulting in an exponential amplification (2n) of the target DNA.

PCR: Pro-Tips & Applications

Pro-Tip: Designing Perfect Primers

A successful PCR depends on good primers. They should be:

  • Specific Length: Typically 18-25 nucleotides.
  • Balanced: About 40-60% GC content (Guanine/Cytosine) for good binding.
  • Not Self-Complementary: Must avoid binding to themselves (hairpins) or each other (primer-dimers).

Common Applications

Forensics: Amplifying tiny DNA samples from a crime scene for identification.
Disease Diagnosis: Detecting the presence of viral or bacterial DNA (like for COVID-19 or HIV).
Research: Cloning genes, studying gene expression, and sequencing ancient DNA.

3. Transcription

Reading the Blueprint (DNA to RNA)

Replication vs. Transcription

While replication copies the *entire* genome, transcription is selective. It copies only specific genes or groups of genes into RNA. It uses RNA Polymerase, doesn't need a primer, and uses Uracil (U) instead of Thymine (T).

Key Concept: Template vs. Coding Strand

RNA Polymerase "reads" the template strand (3' to 5') to build the new RNA. The other DNA strand, the coding strand, is not read, but its sequence (with T changed to U) will perfectly match the new RNA.

Key Concept: The RNA Family

  • mRNA (Messenger): The "instructions" that get translated into protein. (5-10%)
  • tRNA (Transfer): The "adaptor" that carries amino acids to the ribosome. (10-20%)
  • rRNA (Ribosomal): The "factory" component; forms the core of the ribosome. (60-80%)

Prokaryotic Transcription: A 3-Step Process

1.

Initiation

The RNA Polymerase holoenzyme (core enzyme + sigma factor) binds to a promoter region on the DNA (at the -10 "Pribnow box" and -35 sequences) and unwinds it.

2.

Elongation

The sigma factor releases, and the core enzyme moves along the template strand, synthesizing a complementary RNA strand in the 5' to 3' direction.

3.

Termination

Synthesis stops at a terminator signal. This can be Rho-independent (an RNA hairpin loop) or Rho-dependent (a protein factor that pushes the polymerase off).

Eukaryotic Upgrades

More Complexity, More Control

  • Multiple Polymerases: Eukaryotes use three (Pol I for rRNA, Pol II for mRNA, Pol III for tRNA).
  • Transcription Factors: Pol II requires many general transcription factors (like TFIIs) to bind the TATA box promoter.
  • Regulation: Gene expression is controlled by enhancers and silencers, DNA sequences that can be thousands of bases away from the gene.
  • Cistrons: Eukaryotic mRNA is monocistronic (one gene per mRNA). Prokaryotic mRNA is often polycistronic (multiple genes per mRNA).

Post-Transcriptional Modification

In eukaryotes, the initial "primary transcript" (hnRNA) must be processed before it can leave the nucleus:

  • 5' Capping: A special 7-methylguanosine cap is added to the 5' end for protection and ribosome recognition.
  • 3' Poly(A) Tail: A long chain of adenine bases is added to the 3' end for stability.
  • Splicing: Non-coding regions (introns) are removed, and coding regions (exons) are joined together by a complex called the spliceosome.

Preparation: Charging the tRNA

Before translation can even begin, the "adaptor" tRNA molecules must be loaded with their correct amino acids. This is a crucial, high-energy step.

Aminoacyl-tRNA Synthetase

This is the *real* hero of translation. It's an enzyme that "reads" both an amino acid and a tRNA, ensuring a perfect match.

  • Step 1 (Activation): The enzyme uses ATP to attach an amino acid, creating a high-energy aminoacyl-AMP.
  • Step 2 (Charging): The enzyme transfers the amino acid to the 3' end of the correct tRNA, releasing AMP.
  • The Result: A "charged" aminoacyl-tRNA, ready for the ribosome.

4. Translation

Building the Machine (RNA to Protein)

Translation is the final step, where the genetic code on the mRNA is read to synthesize a polypeptide (a protein). This entire process is orchestrated by the ribosome.

The "Tools" for Building

mRNA (Messenger RNA)

The "Instructions." It carries the genetic code from the DNA in the form of three-letter "codons" (e.g., AUG, GUC, UAA).

Charged tRNA (Transfer RNA)

The "Adaptor." One end has an anticodon that matches an mRNA codon. The other end carries its specific amino acid.

Ribosome (rRNA + Protein)

The "Factory." It has two subunits (large and small) and three sites: A (Aminoacyl), P (Peptidyl), and E (Exit).

1. Initiation

The small ribosomal subunit binds the mRNA. In prokaryotes, it finds the Shine-Dalgarno sequence. In eukaryotes, it scans from the 5' cap for the Kozak sequence. The first tRNA (carrying fMet in prokaryotes, Met in eukaryotes) binds the start codon (AUG) in the P site, and the large subunit joins.

2. Elongation

A new charged tRNA lands in the A site. The ribosome's 23S rRNA (a ribozyme) catalyzes a peptide bond, transferring the growing polypeptide from the P-site tRNA to the A-site tRNA. The ribosome then translocates, moving the tRNAs to the E and P sites.

3. Termination

When a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) enters the A site, no tRNA matches. Instead, a release factor binds, causing the ribosome to add a water molecule to the polypeptide, freeing it. The ribosomal subunits and mRNA then dissociate.

Pro-Tip: Common Inhibitors

These processes are common targets for antibiotics and toxins:

  • Rifampicin: (Antibiotic) Inhibits prokaryotic RNA polymerase.
  • α-Amanitin: (Mushroom Toxin) A deadly inhibitor of eukaryotic RNA Polymerase II.
  • Actinomycin D: Binds DNA and blocks the movement of RNA polymerase (inhibits transcription).

Breaking the Dogma: Reverse Transcription

The central dogma (DNA → RNA → Protein) is not absolute. Retroviruses (like HIV) use an enzyme called Reverse Transcriptase to synthesize DNA *from* an RNA template (RNA → DNA). This viral DNA can then integrate into the host's genome.
